Course: (INFO 1300) Introductory Design and Programming for the Web - Cornell University

Partners: Joyce Jin, Aaryaman Mishr, Stephen Owusu, Claire Yun

Tools: Atom, Adobe Photoshop, and GitHub

ABSTRACT

Our client is "Anything Goes," the only musical troupe on Cornell's campus. The group consists of members from all class years who are dedicated to keeping the enthusiasm of musical theatre alive. The club wants an informative website to keep their audience updated about their shows throughout the semester. The group also seeks an interactive website which will allow them to connect with students who are interested in joining or auditioning as well as anyone interested in attending their shows.

01 –– PROBLEM

The client wishes to:

  • Legitimize their group to the public.
  • Have a website that is cohesive yet reflects their theme changes each semester.
  • Increase ticket sales.
  • Have a way for people to express their interest in joining or auditioning.

02 –– GOALS

  • Create a fun and professional looking website that suits the troupe.
  • Create a page dedicated to semester productions to reflect theme changes.
  • Come up with a more efficient way for ticketing.
  • Include forms to allow users to express their interest in auditioning or joining the listserve.

03 –– TARGET AUDIENCE

The profile of the site's potential users ranges from students who may be interested in joining the troupe, to friends and family interested in attending one of their productions. After hosting interviews with potential users, we were able to pinpoint common needs held by the audience. The majority of potential users:

  • Want to learn more about the troupe.
  • Want to purchase tickets.
  • Are interested in joining the listserve.
  • Need a website with easy Navigation.
  • Need a website that is simple and not overbearing with irrelevant text or content.
  • Need to be able to find information easily.
  • Need to be able to contact the group.

04 –– FIRST ITERATION

We developed the structure of our website in accordance with feedback comments from meetings with clients and potential users. Additionally, in examining existing platforms, we noticed that many of the professionally made sites had landing pages. For this reason, we decided to include one in the making of our website.

  • Landing page
    • ↳ Audition Form
  • ↳ Home
  • ↳ About
    • ↳ Cast
    • ↳ Band
    • ↳ Audition Form
  • ↳ Production
    • ↳ Performances
  • ↳ Gallery

05 –– SECOND ITERATION

The features we added in our second prototype include:

  • A ticket button that produces a pop-up form on every page.
  • Hover text over cast and band member portraits.
  • Media queries to ensure responsivity across various devices and screen resolutions.

06 –– THIRD ITERATION

For our third iteration, we decided to stay true to our original idea of maintaining a monochromatic color scheme across the site and allowing users to hover above banner images to reveal the semester's production theme colors. This design choice makes it possible to maintain a cohesive site and simultaneously reflect unique themes.

07 –– USER TESTING

We conducted our user tests by selecting participants that belong to our client's target audience. We gave them a series of tasks to complete (fully testing the flow and navigation of the entire site), recorded and took notes on their results/performances, and gathered feedback to determined what re-design choices we would have to make. From these tests, we decided to simplify certain features on our website. For instance, we had initially included a hidden 'Tickets' form on every page which responded as a pop up to user's hovering above any 'Ticket' button or image. However, users found this a bit "annoying." For simplicity, we decided to just create a separate tab for ticketing where the user could navigate to and fill out the form if they desired to do so. This kept the site's content more organized and eliminated the redundancy of our previous solution.

08 –– FINAL

There were some other features that did not make it onto the final website. In addition to the changes already previously stated, we had also discussed having each individual image in the Gallery's slideshow contain blurbs that would appear when users hovered above any given image. Because I solely worked on the gallery page, I ran into some technical difficulties while trying to implement these features. The main problem was that it was difficult to sync the exact moment the image stopped sliding automatically with the moment in which the hover text appeared with information about that specific image. I first tried to break this problem up and come up with some solutions. I tried writing script in JavaScript that would pause the slideshow when a user hovered above an image. I then used CSS to create an opaque block element with text inside that appears while the slideshow is paused. However, it became increasingly difficult to keep track of this when the previous button was hit. In addition, a lot of the formatting was not showing up as planned (across browsers and devices) and at the time I just did not have the time or knowledge of JavaScript needed to perfect the gallery idea before the submission deadline and therefore decided to settle for a little less. The gallery is fully functional with an automated looping of the images (on screens too small to use control buttons).

Finally, in discussing deployment options with the client, we suggested using cloud platforms such as Heroku to host the website (should they find the cost of purchasing a domain and managing the site an inconvenience.) As taught in this course, it is important that we ensure the client has some knowledge of the process of deployment and give them the opportunity to make choices that are most convenient for them.
